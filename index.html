Understanding Propositions, Logical Operators, and Truth Tables

Introduction to Logic

What is Logic?

Logic is technically defined as "the science or study of how to evaluate arguments and reasoning." It helps us differentiate correct reasoning from poor reasoning and is fundamental to mathematics and computer science.

Mathematical Logic

Mathematical logic (or symbolic logic) is a branch of mathematics with close connections to computer science. It includes both the mathematical study of logic and the applications of formal logic to other areas of mathematics.

Four Divisions of Mathematical Logic

  • Set Theory - Study of collections of objects
  • Model Theory - Study of mathematical structures
  • Recursion Theory - Study of computability
  • Proof Theory - Study of formal proofs

Applications

Logical reasoning is used in:

  • Mathematics to prove theorems
  • Computer science to verify correctness of programs
  • Philosophy to analyze arguments
  • Everyday decision-making and critical thinking

Propositions

A proposition (or statement) is a declarative sentence which is either true or false, but not both. The truth value of a proposition is its truth or falsity.

Examples of Propositions

✓ Valid Propositions:

"Manila is the capital of the Philippines." - This is TRUE, therefore it is a proposition.

"2 + 2 = 5." - This is FALSE, but still a proposition.

✗ Not Propositions:

  • "What day is it?" - This is a question; it cannot be true or false.
  • "Help me, please." - This is a command; it cannot be true or false.
  • "He is handsome." - The subject "he" is not specified, making it neither true nor false.

Types of Propositions

Simple (Atomic) Propositions

A proposition that cannot be broken down into smaller propositions.

Example: "The sky is blue."

Compound Propositions

A proposition composed of two or more simple propositions connected by logical connectives such as "and," "or," "if then," "not," "if and only if," and "exclusive-or."

Example: "The sky is blue AND the grass is green."

Propositional Variables

In formal logic, we use variables like p, q, and r to represent propositions. This allows us to study the structure of logical arguments without focusing on specific content.
